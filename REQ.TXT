A data analysis project exploring Zomato restaurant data to uncover insights about ratings, pricing, online ordering trends, dining types, and customer votes.

---

## 📁 Dataset Overview

**File:** `Zomato_data_.csv`  
**Rows:** ~148 restaurants  
**Columns:** 7

| Column | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| `name` | Name of the restaurant |
| `online_order` | Whether the restaurant accepts online orders (`Yes` / `No`) |
| `book_table` | Whether table booking is available (`Yes` / `No`) |
| `rate` | Customer rating out of 5 (e.g., `4.1/5`) |
| `votes` | Total number of customer votes |
| `approx_cost(for two people)` | Approximate dining cost for two people (in ₹) |
| `listed_in(type)` | Restaurant category (`Buffet`, `Cafes`, `Dining`, `other`) |

---

## 🔍 Project Objectives

- Clean and preprocess the raw Zomato dataset
- Analyze restaurant ratings and vote distributions
- Compare online ordering vs. non-ordering restaurants
- Explore the impact of table booking on ratings
- Identify the most affordable and premium dining options
- Visualize trends across restaurant types and cost ranges

---

## 🛠️ Tech Stack

- **Python 3.x**
- **Pandas** — Data manipulation and cleaning
- **NumPy** — Numerical computations
- **Matplotlib** — Plotting and visualization
- **Seaborn** — Statistical charts and heatmaps
- **Jupyter Notebook** — Interactive analysis environment

## 📌 Key Insights (Preview)

- **~60%** of restaurants in the dataset do **not** accept online orders.
- **Dining** is the most common restaurant type, making up the majority of listings.
- Restaurants with **table booking** tend to have slightly higher ratings on average.
- Cost for two ranges from **₹100 to ₹950**, indicating a wide variety of dining options.
- High vote counts don't always guarantee the highest ratings — some hidden gems exist.
